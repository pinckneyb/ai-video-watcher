Pattern: Subcuticular

1. Consistent dermal bites (running path): There is no continuous intradermal track; the closure was executed with discrete transcutaneous interrupted bites rather than a running subcuticular path. Bite size varied between the superior and inferior half with no dermal continuity. 1/5 developing
2. Opposing entry/exit symmetry: When bites were taken, opposing entry/exit points were generally mirrored across the incision with perpendicular needle handling, yielding acceptable bilateral spacing despite the non‑subcuticular plane. 3/5 proficient
3. No unintended surface breaches: Multiple mid‑line epidermal breaches are present, meeting the threshold for a non‑subcuticular designation (breaches at ~90–104 s and ~120–134 s; additional breach at ~135–149 s). 1/5 developing
4. Gentle tissue handling: Tissue handling was gentle with light Adson use for eversion and controlled needle arcs; minimal crush or torque on the skin edges. 4/5 exemplary
5. Square, secure knots: Instrument ties appear square and adequately seated with appropriate tail trimming, but knots are external/on‑line rather than buried or lateral. 3/5 proficient
6. Flat, well-approximated skin: Final skin appearance shows dimpling/ridging at stitch sites with mild residual gapping; not the flat line expected from a subcuticular closure. 2/5 competent
7. Economy of time and motion: Efficiency is limited by unnecessary diversion to a separate wound and multiple passes before committing to ties; repeated re‑grasping prolongs the task. 2/5 competent

Average Score: 2.3/5 competent

Summative Comment: This performance does not meet subcuticular standards. The active incision was closed using transcutaneous simple‑interrupted stitches with multiple epidermal punctures and external knots, rather than a continuous intradermal track. The surface result predictably shows dimpling and mild ridging, not the flat approximation required of a subcuticular closure. There are positives: needle control is stable, entries are perpendicular with reasonable bilateral symmetry, and tissue handling is gentle with square instrument ties. To achieve a true subcuticular repair, start with a buried dermal anchor, keep the needle nearly parallel to the skin, and advance in the dermis just deep to the epidermis with equal 5–7 mm bites that mirror the prior exit. Maintain consistent depth, light tension to avoid ridging, and finish with a buried/aberrdeen termination or lateral tie off the line. Stay with one wound, minimize regrasping, and convert each motion into forward progress.
